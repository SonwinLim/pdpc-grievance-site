**Subject: Follow-up on my PDPC complaint — an update, and a question for Parliament**

Dear [MP name],

I am writing to update you on the situation I raised with you last year regarding my complaint against PDPC.

I still do not know what happened to me on the night of the accident, because the footage that would have shown me was denied, and then destroyed.

Recently I discovered that, rather than addressing the substance of my complaint, PDPC redesigned its enforcement-decisions website so that it is no longer possible to filter published cases by the type of obligation breached (PDPC's own page: https://www.pdpc.gov.sg/organisations/regulations-decisions/regulatory-guidance?page=1&sort=latest). I had previously pointed out that PDPC has never once found a breach of the Access Obligation across its entire published enforcement record. That has not changed.

In response, I rebuilt the missing filter myself: https://pdpaaccessrights.sg/enforcement-index.html. I have also published a full account of what went wrong in my case, so that PDPC cannot simply let the matter be forgotten: https://pdpaaccessrights.sg/index.html.

I also found that, apart from my own case, PDPC has never investigated a single CCTV access-denial complaint in the entire history of its published decisions. Having seen how my own case was ultimately resolved, once it could no longer be avoided, it is not hard to infer why that number stays at zero. The attached chart shows this pattern against every other obligation PDPC enforces: Access stands at zero breach findings, while Protection alone accounts for 204.

Beyond my own grievances, I have always maintained that PDPC's own conclusion in its report effectively renders almost all personal data captured by CCTV as non-personal data. That conclusion contradicts the PDPA itself, PDPC's own published Guidelines, and PDPC's own past findings.

The PDPA's definition of personal data has more than one limb for a reason, in the same way a bare membership number is treated as personal data once it can be combined with other information an organisation holds. PDPC's own Advisory Guidelines (page 42, para 4.59) go further and state that even blurred or pixelated masking may not be foolproof, because individuals in such images can still be identifiable. In my case, I was identified from that very same footage, on-site, within minutes, outside of any formal PDPA process. PDPC then used that same footage to conclude it was not personal data at all.

I would like to ask for your help with this question: it may be understandable, if unfortunate, for a regulator to under-enforce a right under the PDPA. But is it overreach, or worse, for the regulator itself to actively deny a citizen that right, under the very law it exists to uphold? The PDPA is the reason PDPC exists.

Since it is the World Cup now, I will borrow an analogy: this is PDPC's Hand of God moment. I asked the referee, PDPC, to review the play. It denied every request to look at the evidence, and let the goal stand, denying me the outcome the rules required. Today's game has VAR, cameras, replay, more evidence than Maradona's referee ever had, and still the referee looked away. Worse, in real football, that referee can be reviewed and held to account by someone else. Here, the enforcer sits in judgment of itself. Nothing happens.

I sent PDPC and IMDA the new website (https://pdpaaccessrights.sg) on 21 June 2026, as I have with all my previous correspondence. I have received no feedback and no explanation. If I am mistaken anywhere on the site, I apologize, and I remain open to correction. But every claim on it is backed by emails, PDPC's own guidance, the PDPA itself, and PDPC's own past rulings.

I would like to ask you directly: is it right that a citizen's rights under the law can be denied this way, by the very body appointed to protect them? And who made that call?

Yours sincerely,
Lim Zirui (Ray Lim)
