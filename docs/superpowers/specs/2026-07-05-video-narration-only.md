## Scene 1 — The Hook [0:00–0:30] · 30s · clean/systemic

In September twenty twenty-five, Mr Zhulkarnain Abdul Rahim asked the minister responsible for the PDPA a simple question.

When someone requests their own CCTV footage under the Personal Data Protection Act, must the organisation preserve that footage while the request is being processed?

Minister Josephine Teo’s answer was clear. Yes. Organisations must have appropriate measures to preserve the requested data. There are criminal penalties for intentional destruction to avoid an access request.

That was said in Parliament on the twenty-second of September twenty twenty-five.

But by then, my footage had already been deleted.

This is what happened when I made my request.

## Scene 2 — The Accident [0:30–1:30] · 60s · hand-drawn

Thirteenth of April twenty twenty-four. Five oh six in the morning. I was riding my motorcycle on Cairnhill Road when a taxi cut into my lane.

The taxi driver gave the first account. He said I had skidded on my own.

I had no memory to contradict him.

The diagnosis came later: a subarachnoid haemorrhage. Bleeding around the brain.

I had no memory of what happened to me. Not the impact. Not the minutes before. Nothing.

What I did know was this: two condominiums flanked the road where the accident happened. Both had CCTV cameras pointing at the street.

When security guards checked the footage, they identified it within minutes. They described to me what had happened. I was then asked to make a formal request to management.

So I did, starting with The Scotts Tower, because that was the more direct front view of the accident.

The taxi cutting into my lane was on video. It existed. It was findable.

That footage was the only record of an event I have no memory of. I needed it for two reasons. One: to understand what happened to me, and to prove I did not skid on my own. Two: to support an insurance claim against the driver.

Both seemed straightforward. Both were not.

## Scene 3 — The Denials [1:30–3:15] · 105s · hand-drawn

Four days after the accident, I walked into the management office of The Scotts Tower. I explained what happened. I asked for the CCTV footage of the road.

They said no. Privacy reasons.

I asked who the data protection officer was. They wouldn’t tell me. I asked what company managed the building. They wouldn’t tell me that either. I asked who I could escalate to. No answer.

I left the building with no company name, no DPO contact, and no escalation path. I had to identify the managing agent myself. I had to track down the DPO myself.

The other condominium, Suites@Cairnhill, began differently. Staff there had allowed me to take a short recording of the CCTV screen. Not through the PDPA process. Just a brief recording, because they could see why I needed to understand what had happened.

After PDPC ignored my request for the more direct front footage from The Scotts Tower, I later tried the other angle. I made a formal request to Suites@Cairnhill for fuller footage. I did not say who had helped me earlier. I was trying to protect the staff.

The managing agent’s DPO then refused in writing. On the twenty-first of May twenty twenty-four, he wrote: “Only by police direct or order the MCST to disclose the footage that MCST is obliged to do so.”

That is not what PDPC’s own guidelines say. The guidelines for management corporations say access may not be limited only to enforcement agencies, such as the police.

I wrote back, citing those guidelines.

The DPO’s reply: “It seems to me you are having difficulty understanding our position.” And he refused to communicate with me further.

Neither condominium cited a single ground from the law. The PDPA lists the only legal reasons a request can be refused — section twenty-one, subsection three, and the Fifth Schedule. Privacy as a blanket reason is not on that list. Police-only access is not on that list.

And later, the short recording from Suites@Cairnhill, the one I had taken only because staff helped me, was used by PDPC to say the footage was not personal data.

## Scene 4 — The Deletion Window [3:15–4:00] · 45s · hand-drawn

While I was trying to find out who managed the building, the clock was running.

At first, I was told the footage was kept for many months. That was my understanding.

So when Knight Frank later said no footage had been captured, I became alarmed. Because I knew that was not true. Security guards had already identified the footage. They had described what happened.

That was when I began to suspect the problem was not that the footage did not exist. The problem was that access could be denied until the footage disappeared.

A new management team later claimed the retention cycle was twenty to thirty days. PDPC eventually said seventeen days, and accepted that figure without verifying it.

Seventeen days. That was the window.

And that date mattered. PDPC later treated Knight Frank’s request to the MCST as the access-request date. Then it treated the auto-deletion as happening before the MCST’s formal refusal.

Under that logic, section twenty-two A became useless. The footage could disappear during the live access process, and deletion was still not treated as a breach.

I was still trying to find out the company name on day four.

## Scene 5 — Filing With PDPC [4:00–5:50] · 110s · hand-drawn

So I did what the law says to do. I filed a formal complaint with the Personal Data Protection Commission.

For The Scotts Tower, I explained the verbal refusal on April seventeenth, my written follow-up on April twenty-fifth, the missing company name, the missing DPO contact, and the claim that no footage had been captured.

At that point, I did not know PDPC would later say the footage had already been overwritten on April thirtieth.

What I did know was this: the footage had been identified. Knight Frank’s “no footage” claim was false. And I was worried that The Scotts Tower MCST or Knight Frank could deny access by letting the video disappear.

I told PDPC that.

PDPC did not preserve or verify the more direct front footage. Instead, I was told to go to the police.

Only after that did I try the other angle from Suites@Cairnhill. When its managing agent refused on the “police-only” ground, I filed that complaint with PDPC as well.

And then.

Nothing meaningful.

No preservation action. No verification. Instead, PDPC came up with reasons on behalf of the condominiums, without checking the core facts.

And none of those reasons addressed what the condominiums had actually said. The Scotts Tower had said privacy, then no footage. Suites@Cairnhill had said police-only access.

Months passed. Eventually, there was a response, but not the one the law seemed to promise.

My Member of Parliament intervened. Twice. PDPC did not move.

I appealed. PDPC did not change its position. Not on privacy. Not on “police-only.” Not on deletion.

After I proved the data had been deleted, and the police confirmed they had no video either, PDPC’s only deflection had run out. They had no other reasons to reject me.

What they did instead was open some kind of investigation into TST’s data deletion. I do not know which obligation it was opened under. I do not know what they looked at. I just know it was not the right I had asked about.

That is the pattern. The right to retrieve my own data was treated as not worth investigating. The deletion of that same data, after it was gone, was treated as worth investigating.

## Scene 6 — The Clarity Test [5:50–6:50] · 60s · mixed (hand-drawn → systemic)

Eventually, PDPC issued its findings. Two cases. Two sets of reasoning.

For Suites@Cairnhill, they said the CCTV footage did not contain “personal data.”

Here is how they made that determination. They relied on my own evidence — a short handphone recording of the CCTV screen, taken at the management office, after staff had helped me view what their camera captured.

That recording existed because staff helped me. I did not disclose that earlier help when I later made the formal request, because I did not want to get the staff into trouble. But later, PDPC used the recording against me.

PDPC said the rider was only a silhouette. No facial features. No motorcycle licence plate. Therefore, not personal data.

But that is not what the statute says. The PDPA does not say a face or licence plate must be visible. It defines personal data as data about an individual who can be identified from that data or from that data and other information.

And there was other information. The guards had identified the footage within minutes. The request gave the date, time, place, and who I was. The whole reason I was asking was because that camera captured my accident.

Identifiability. Not clarity.

This is the moment to understand. PDPC took my own evidence. Used it against me. And the test they applied — the clarity test — does not exist in the legislation. It was invented. By the regulator. To dismiss my case.

In football, there is a famous goal scored by Diego Maradona — his hand made contact with the ball. The referee missed it. The goal stood. Afterwards, Maradona called it “the hand of God.”

PDPC’s clarity test is the regulatory equivalent. They scored a goal with something never in the rules. The legal test was identifiability. They replaced it with visual clarity. Then they declared victory. And moved on.

## Scene 7 — Two Cases, Two Invented Reasonings [6:50–8:05] · 75s · clean/systemic

Let me show you what PDPC did, side by side.

First condominium — Suites@Cairnhill. The DPO said: “Only by police direct/order the MCST to disclose the footage.” PDPC said: “The footage contains no personal data.” The condominium never said that. PDPC invented that reason for them.

Second condominium — The Scotts Tower. I made a verbal request on April seventeenth. I was refused on privacy grounds, and given no DPO contact, no company name, no escalation path. On April twenty-fifth, I followed up in writing. When challenged, they later claimed no footage existed. That was false — PDPC’s own decision says the footage existed until April thirtieth.

But PDPC’s preservation analysis did not give legal weight to either of those earlier events. Not the April seventeenth verbal refusal by the managing agent. Not my April twenty-fifth written access request. Instead, the timeline shifted to what happened inside the condominium: the managing agent asked the MCST on April twenty-ninth. The footage was overwritten on April thirtieth. The formal refusal came on May second.

That is the twist. My request disappears. The managing agent’s earlier refusal disappears. The false “no footage” statement disappears. The warning that the footage might be deleted disappears. And s.22A becomes useless, because deletion before the formal refusal becomes no breach.

In both cases, PDPC substituted its own reasoning for what the condominiums actually said. And in neither case did they test the actual stated grounds against the law. Section twenty-one, subsection three, together with the Fifth Schedule — these are the only provisions that authorise an organisation to lawfully refuse an access request.

“Privacy” is not listed. “Police-only” is not listed. PDPC never even checked.

## Scene 8 — Nine Questions, One Response [8:05–9:40] · 95s · clean/systemic

I wrote to PDPC with nine questions. Specific, legal questions across six sections of the PDPA.

Which section authorises the clarity test? Why were the condominiums’ actual reasons never tested against the Fifth Schedule? Why did the April seventeenth verbal refusal not matter? Why did my April twenty-fifth written request not trigger preservation before the footage disappeared?

Why did “many months,” what security told me, become seventeen days? Why was a request handled by the managing agent treated as legally different from the MCST’s own responsibility, when section four, subsection two removes most direct obligations from a data intermediary, and section four, subsection three puts the same obligation back on the organisation as if it processed the data itself?

Those questions were never answered.

There was a repeated line: “The Guidelines are not determinative. The PDPA takes precedence.” But that does not answer the question. It does not say which guideline. It does not say which section of the PDPA. It does not say what the conflict is.

And when PDPC did try to answer a limited point, like the definition of personal data, they did not explain the original reasoning. They introduced a new idea: reasonableness. As if identifiability depends on whether it is reasonable to identify me from the footage.

But the security guards had already found the footage within minutes. They knew the date. They knew the time. They knew the location. They knew who was asking. That is the real-world identifiability that PDPC ignored.

The written findings were eventually released. When I posted questions on the reasoning, PDPC did not answer on the merits. Months later, more reasonings appeared, including a “reasonableness” gloss on identifiability, without explaining the original gap. PDPC also declined to publish the report online promptly. That only happened after I escalated. On June twenty-third, twenty twenty-five, PDPC wrote: “Decisions and undertakings are generally published on the microsite within the year. We are unable to commit to a fixed date at this point.”

So the public could not see the reasoning. My questions were not answered on the merits. The findings were not promptly on the public record. And PDPC treated the matter as closed.

But the TST investigation had already proven that the data had been deleted. That was no longer in dispute. Even so, the officer handling my case continued with flawed reasonings for my Suites complaint, closing it on the basis that it was similar to TST. That could not stand. TST was wrong. Saying Suites was similar to a wrong decision was not a reason at all. I asked for the decision to be reopened. What I got in return was rude and unbecoming behavior expected of a civil servant.

## Scene 9 — IMDA — The Oversight That Didn’t Oversee [9:40–11:20] · 100s · clean/systemic

I did not want to complain about PDPC. I went there because I needed my footage.

In September twenty twenty-four, I escalated to IMDA. I emailed Mr Lew Chuen Hong, IMDA’s Chief Executive. I copied the PDPC Commissioner. And because I did not know which department would handle my feedback, I sent the email to three IMDA branches at the same time.

The complaint ran for eleven months. I sent twelve emails. The last was on the fourteenth of August twenty twenty-five. During the IMDA investigation, my complaint was leaked back to the PDPC officers under review.

On the twentieth of August twenty twenty-five, Ms Yeong Wan Ling, IMDA’s Head of Internal Audit, wrote to me. Her finding: PDPC and its officers “did not commit any wrongful practices.”

She endorsed PDPC’s reasoning. She said PDPC had directed me to inform the police — to avoid interfering with police investigations. That was her stated rationale for why nothing was wrong.

That rationale does not exist in the PDPA. The only grounds for refusing access are section twenty-one, subsection three, and the Fifth Schedule. “Active police investigation” is not listed. It was an excuse. IMDA endorsed it.

IMDA also said PDPC had “acted in accordance with its protocols.” And it identified “certain areas of improvement” — without naming them.

The findings were “final and conclusive.” The investigation was closed.

## Scene 10 — The 384 Cases (Maradona Beat 2) [11:20–12:32] · 72s · clean/systemic

By this point, I stopped asking whether my case was handled wrongly. I started asking a different question. How many cases like mine has PDPC ever found a breach on?

PDPC’s website used to let you filter enforcement decisions by obligation type. At some point, that filter disappeared. So I went through the entire register myself. Every decision. Every finding. Three hundred and eighty-four cases, compiled as of June twenty twenty-six.

The Access Obligation — section twenty-one of the PDPA, the right of a citizen to access their own personal data — has produced zero breach findings. Zero. Across three hundred and eighty-four published enforcement actions. Not one.

The dominant pattern of PDPC enforcement is data protection and consent — organisations mishandling data they hold. The right of a citizen to retrieve their own data from an organisation? That is the least enforced provision in the entire register.

Across those three hundred and eighty-four cases, only two involve a complainant demanding access to their own CCTV data and being refused. Both are mine. Both from the same accident on Cairnhill Road. Both dismissed without a finding that the Access Obligation was breached.

The clarity test explains this. If face or licence plate must be visible for CCTV footage to qualify as personal data — and that standard exists nowhere in the statute — then any organisation can deny an access request by asserting the footage is too unclear. And the Complainant has no tools to challenge that assertion.

After the nineteen eighty-six World Cup quarter-final, Maradona was asked about his goal. He said: “It was a little bit the hand of God, and a little bit the head of Maradona.”

Not quite admitting. Not quite denying.

PDPC’s one-line reply — “The Guidelines are not determinative; the PDPA takes precedence” — to nine legal questions about the merits. Same energy. They cannot say what really happened. So they give the same non-answer to each question, and declare the file closed.

## Scene 11 — The Only Explanation [12:32–14:00] · 88s · clean/systemic

Here is what I have learned, going through every document, every decision, every response.

Ordinary regulatory failure is uneven. Mistakes in different directions. Inconsistent outcomes. Different responses to different challenges.

What is documented here — across two PDPC cases, an IMDA review, nine legal questions, three hundred and eighty-four enforcement actions — is not uneven. Every decision. At every level. In the same direction.

PDPC invented reasoning for the condominiums. IMDA stamped “no wrongful practices” without opening the envelope. Nine legal questions got one line. Zero breach findings across three hundred and eighty-four cases.

This is the shape of a policy. Not necessarily an explicit one. Nobody needs to have written it down.

Here is the principle that distinguishes enforcement discretion from what happened here.

Any body can decide to actively enforce something, or not, according to the circumstances of the day. That is a regulator’s discretion. What no body can do is actively decide to deny the rights of a citizen or resident, and reinterpret the law to protect the perpetrators of that denial.

That is what happened here. An active decision to deny — and an active decision to invent grounds to justify the denial.

The clarity test is the legal mechanism. The zero-breach history is the outcome. The non-engagement is what follows when a regulator cannot defend its position on the merits.
