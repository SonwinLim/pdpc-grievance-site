## Scene 1 — The Hook [0:00–0:30] · 30s · clean/systemic
---

**What happened:**
On 22 September 2025, Minister Josephine Teo told Parliament that the PDPA requires organisations to preserve personal data while access requests are processed, with criminal penalties for intentional destruction. In the Complainant’s case — the only two CCTV-access-refusal complaints across 384 published PDPC enforcement actions — the footage was deleted. PDPC found no preservation breach and no Access Obligation breach. Both cannot be true. One of them must be wrong.

**Narration:**
In September twenty twenty-five, Mr Zhulkarnain Abdul Rahim asked the minister responsible for the PDPA a simple question.

When someone requests their own CCTV footage under the Personal Data Protection Act, must the organisation preserve that footage while the request is being processed?

Minister Josephine Teo’s answer was clear. Yes. Organisations must have appropriate measures to preserve the requested data. There are criminal penalties for intentional destruction to avoid an access request.

That was said in Parliament on the twenty-second of September twenty twenty-five.

But by then, my footage had already been deleted.

This is what happened when I made my request.

## Scene 2 — The Accident [0:30–1:30] · 60s · hand-drawn
---

**What happened:**
On 13 April 2024, at approximately 5:06 AM, the Complainant was injured when a taxi cut into his lane on Cairnhill Road, Singapore. The taxi driver told the authorities the Complainant had skidded on his own. The Complainant had no memory of the accident and could not contradict that account. The impact caused a subarachnoid haemorrhage, bleeding around the brain, which was discovered later. The two condominiums flanking the road, The Scotts Tower and Suites@Cairnhill, both had CCTV cameras covering the road. Security staff at both condominiums identified the relevant footage within minutes of checking, described what had happened, and the Complainant was asked to make formal requests to management, which he later did.

**Narration:**
Thirteenth of April twenty twenty-four. Five oh six in the morning. I was riding my motorcycle on Cairnhill Road when a taxi cut into my lane.

The taxi driver gave the first account. He said I had skidded on my own.

I had no memory to contradict him.

The diagnosis came later: a subarachnoid haemorrhage. Bleeding around the brain.

I had no memory of what happened to me. Not the impact. Not the minutes before. Nothing.

What I did know was this: two condominiums flanked the road where the accident happened. Both had CCTV cameras pointing at the street.

When security guards checked the footage, they identified it within minutes. They described to me what had happened. I was then asked to make a formal request to management.

So I did, starting with The Scotts Tower, because that was the more direct front view of the accident.

The taxi cutting into my lane was on video. It existed. It was findable.

That footage was the only record of an event I have no memory of. I needed it for two reasons. One: to understand what happened to me, and to prove I did not skid on my own. Two: to support an insurance claim against the driver.

Both seemed straightforward. Both were not.

## Scene 3 — The Denials [1:30–3:15] · 105s · hand-drawn
---

**What happened:**
The Scotts Tower (MCST 4599) refused access on 17 April 2024, citing “privacy” as a blanket reason. When challenged, they claimed no footage existed, refuted by PDPC’s own subsequent finding that footage existed until 30 April. The managing agent refused to provide the company name, the DPO contact, or any escalation path. The Complainant had to independently identify the managing agent and track down the DPO himself. At Suites@Cairnhill (MCST 3615), staff had earlier allowed the Complainant to take a short handphone recording of the CCTV screen outside the formal PDPA process. After PDPC ignored the request for the more direct front footage from The Scotts Tower, the Complainant later made a formal access request to Suites@Cairnhill for fuller footage, but did not disclose the earlier staff assistance in order to protect the staff. The managing agent’s DPO, Mohamed Nasir Mustaffa of Property Facility Services Pte Ltd, refused on 21 May 2024, stating in writing: “Only by police direct/order the MCST to disclose the footage that MCST is obliged to do so.” PDPC’s own Advisory Guidelines for Management Corporations state that access may not be limited only to enforcement agencies such as the police. When the Complainant cited the guidelines, the DPO responded: “It seems to me you are having difficulty understanding our position” and refused further communication. The short recording later became the material PDPC used to conclude that the footage was “not personal data.” Neither condominium cited a lawful refusal ground from s.21(3) or the Fifth Schedule of the PDPA.

**Narration:**
Four days after the accident, I walked into the management office of The Scotts Tower. I explained what happened. I asked for the CCTV footage of the road.

They said no. Privacy reasons.

I asked who the data protection officer was. They wouldn’t tell me. I asked what company managed the building. They wouldn’t tell me that either. I asked who I could escalate to. No answer.

I left the building with no company name, no DPO contact, and no escalation path. I had to identify the managing agent myself. I had to track down the DPO myself.

The other condominium, Suites@Cairnhill, began differently. Staff there had allowed me to take a short recording of the CCTV screen. Not through the PDPA process. Just a brief recording, because they could see why I needed to understand what had happened.

After PDPC ignored my request for the more direct front footage from The Scotts Tower, I later tried the other angle. I made a formal request to Suites@Cairnhill for fuller footage. I did not say who had helped me earlier. I was trying to protect the staff.

The managing agent’s DPO then refused in writing. On the twenty-first of May twenty twenty-four, he wrote: “Only by police direct or order the MCST to disclose the footage that MCST is obliged to do so.”

That is not what PDPC’s own guidelines say. The guidelines for management corporations say access may not be limited only to enforcement agencies, such as the police.

I wrote back, citing those guidelines.

The DPO’s reply: “It seems to me you are having difficulty understanding our position.” And he refused to communicate with me further.

Neither condominium cited a single ground from the law. The PDPA lists the only legal reasons a request can be refused — section twenty-one, subsection three, and the Fifth Schedule. Privacy as a blanket reason is not on that list. Police-only access is not on that list.

And later, the short recording from Suites@Cairnhill, the one I had taken only because staff helped me, was used by PDPC to say the footage was not personal data.

## Scene 4 — The Deletion Window [3:15–4:00] · 45s · hand-drawn
---

**What happened:**
At the time of the access requests, The Scotts Tower security guards told the Complainant the CCTV footage was kept for “many months.” That was the Complainant’s working understanding. He became alarmed when Knight Frank later declared that no footage had been captured, despite security staff having already identified and described the footage. The Complainant suspected access might be denied through deletion. A new managing agent later said the retention cycle was “20 to 30 days.” PDPC later determined that the footage was overwritten on 30 April 2024, 17 days after the accident, and months before any substantive investigation opened. PDPC accepted this 17-day figure without independent verification. The 17-day date became legally important because PDPC treated Knight Frank’s request to the MCST as the operative access-request date, then treated the auto-deletion as occurring before the MCST’s formal refusal. On that framing, s.22A did not bite, and deletion during the live access process was not treated as a breach.

**Narration:**
While I was trying to find out who managed the building, the clock was running.

Seventeen days. That was the window.

Under that logic, section twenty-two A became useless. The footage could disappear during the live access process, and deletion was still not treated as a breach.

I was still trying to find out the company name on day four.

## Scene 6 — The Clarity Test [5:50–6:50] · 60s · mixed (hand-drawn → systemic)
---

**What happened:**
For MCST 3615 (Suites@Cairnhill), PDPC determined that the CCTV footage did not contain “personal data.” Its public summary says the footage captured only the silhouette of the rider and did not capture identifying features such as facial features or the motorcycle licence plate, and that MCST 3615 did not possess other information to identify the individual. That reasoning is the core problem. The PDPA defines personal data by identifiability, including identification from the data “and other information” to which the organisation has or is likely to have access. Here, the footage had already been identified on-site by security staff, the access request gave the date, time, location, and the requester’s identity, and the short handphone recording existed only because staff had earlier helped the Complainant outside the formal PDPA process. PDPC then used that same recording to say the footage was not personal data. This is Maradona Beat 1: a goal scored with something not in the rules.

**Narration:**
Eventually, PDPC issued its findings. Two cases. Two sets of reasoning.

For Suites@Cairnhill, they said the CCTV footage did not contain “personal data.”

Here is how they made that determination. They relied on my own evidence — a short handphone recording of the CCTV screen, taken at the management office, after staff had helped me view what their camera captured.

That recording existed because staff helped me. I did not disclose that earlier help when I later made the formal request, because I did not want to get the staff into trouble. But later, PDPC used the recording against me.

PDPC said the rider was only a silhouette. No facial features. No motorcycle licence plate. Therefore, not personal data.

But that is not what the statute says. The PDPA does not say a face or licence plate must be visible. It defines personal data as data about an individual who can be identified from that data or from that data and other information.

And there was other information. The guards had identified the footage within minutes. The request gave the date, time, place, and who I was. The whole reason I was asking was because that camera captured my accident.

Identifiability. Not clarity.

This is the moment to understand. PDPC took my own evidence. Used it against me. And the test they applied — the clarity test — does not exist in the legislation. It was invented. By the regulator. To dismiss my case.

In football, there is a famous goal scored by Diego Maradona — his hand made contact with the ball. The referee missed it. The goal stood. Afterwards, Maradona called it “the hand of God.”

PDPC’s clarity test is the regulatory equivalent. They scored a goal with something never in the rules. The legal test was identifiability. They replaced it with visual clarity. Then they declared victory. And moved on.

## Scene 8 — Nine Questions, One Response [8:05–9:40] · 95s · clean/systemic
---

**What happened:**
The Complainant sent PDPC nine legal questions spanning six sections of the PDPA. The core questions were not answered. Section 4(2) removes most direct obligations from a data intermediary processing data for another organisation under written contract, while s.4(3) says the organisation has the same obligation as if the data were processed by the organisation itself. Where PDPC attempted to address limited points, such as the definition of personal data, it did not explain its original reasoning. Instead, it introduced a “reasonableness” gloss into the identifiability analysis while ignoring the practical fact that security staff had found the relevant footage within minutes. The written findings were eventually released to the Complainant after escalation, but public publication remained delayed. On 23 June 2025, PDPC wrote: “Decisions and undertakings are generally published on the microsite within the year. We are unable to commit to a fixed date at this point.” PDPC then treated the matter as closed.

## Scene 9 — IMDA — The Oversight That Didn’t Oversee [9:40–11:20] · 100s · clean/systemic
---

**What happened:**
On 8 September 2024, the Complainant escalated to IMDA by emailing Mr Lew Chuen Hong, IMDA’s Chief Executive. Because the Complainant did not know which IMDA department would handle the complaint, the email was addressed simultaneously to three IMDA branches — the Chief Executive’s office, the Quality Service Manager, and the IMDA whistleblower channel — and CC’d to the PDPC Commissioner. The complaint set out three categories of concern: operational (PDPC delays; communication cut off after the second reply), quality and policy (no enforcement decisions on PDPC’s website for the access obligation, signalling to organisations that they can deny access without consequence), and individual (the officer may not have followed standard procedure). Eight substantive points followed, including: PDPC took more than three months to reach out to the managing agents, by which time the footage had been deleted; PDPC provided justifications on behalf of the agents without supporting evidence; the Member of Parliament intervened twice without resolution; PDPC introduced possible exceptions under the Fifth Schedule and other PDPA clauses without input from the agents themselves. The complaint ran for eleven months — twelve emails in total, the last sent on 14 August 2025. During the IMDA investigation, the complaint materials were leaked back to the PDPC officers under review. On 20 August 2025, Ms Yeong Wan Ling, IMDA’s Head of Internal Audit, wrote to the Complainant. IAU’s finding: PDPC and its officers “did not commit any wrongful practices.” IAU endorsed PDPC’s rationale: PDPC had directed the Complainant to inform the police so as not to interfere with police investigations. IAU also said PDPC had “acted in accordance with its protocols” and identified “certain areas of improvement for PDPC to better serve the public” without naming them. The findings were declared “final and conclusive” and the investigation was closed with no further action.
