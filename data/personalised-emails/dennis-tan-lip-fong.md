Dear Mr Dennis Tan Lip Fong,

Your legal training as Senior Counsel and your consistent engagement with civil-rights questions in this House give you a rare toolkit for the issue I want to put before you. I write precisely because statutory interpretation is your professional vocabulary, and what I am describing is, at its core, a question of law.

In March 2025, PDPC removed a filter from its enforcement-decisions website. That filter had previously allowed segmentation by obligation type. After the redesign, no such segmentation exists. The zero Access Obligation breach findings, a statistic plainly derivable from the published record before the change, became effectively undiscoverable for a regular member of the public. The data was not deleted. It was made inconvenient.

The Maradona Hand-of-God goal remains the most honest framing I can offer. The referee was asked to look. He chose not to. VAR exists. The referee still chose not to. There is no appeal within the match. The goal stands, not because it was correct, but because the mechanism for correction was not used.

Section 21 of the Personal Data Protection Act provides individuals the right to access their personal data. PDPC has stated, in correspondence with me, that its guidelines are not determinative and that the PDPA takes precedence; yet it has found zero breaches of s.21 across 374 published enforcement actions. Section 24, by contrast, has generated active enforcement, including fines at major institutions. The inconsistency between the stated position and the enforcement record is, I submit, a matter that deserves scrutiny from someone with your background.

Mr Lim, I have rebuilt the filter independently at https://pdpaaccessrights.sg/enforcement-index.html so that this data remains publicly accessible. Every Singaporean deserves to know that the Access Obligation, a right written into law, has never produced a published enforcement outcome in the history of the PDPA.

Would you consider scrutinising PDPC's "guidelines not determinative" position from a statutory-interpretation angle?
