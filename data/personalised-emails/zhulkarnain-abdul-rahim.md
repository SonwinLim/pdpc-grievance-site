Dear Zhulkarnain Abdul Rahim,

I would like to thank you for the parliamentary question you submitted on my behalf, which resulted in Written Answer 19596 delivered on 9 September 2025. Your question drew a clear assurance from Minister Josephine Teo that organisations are required to preserve data while access requests are being processed, and that criminal penalties apply for intentional concealment. I am writing to update you on what has happened since that answer was given, and to ask whether you would consider a follow-up question.

Despite that assurance, the Personal Data Protection Commission has not acknowledged any breach in my case. It continued to maintain that the footage was not personal data, a position it has never applied consistently across its published decisions. I subsequently published a full account of the case at pdpaaccessrights.sg, including the enforcement-decisions filter I rebuilt after PDPC removed it from its own website. I have offered to correct any error on the site. I have received no substantive reply.

I still do not know what happened to me that night, because the footage that would have shown me was denied and then destroyed. The Personal Data Protection Commission, which exists to enforce the law, failed to do so in my case. Beyond my case, it has never once found a breach of the Access Obligation across its entire published enforcement record spanning over a decade. I brought this to the Commission's attention. It redesigned its enforcement-website to remove the ability to filter by obligation type. I rebuilt that filter myself and published everything at pdpaaccessrights.sg.

The PDPA's definition of personal data has more than one limb, in the same way a bare membership number is treated as personal data once it can be combined with other information an organisation holds. PDPC's own Advisory Guidelines (page 42, para 4.59) go further and state that even blurred or pixelated masking may not be foolproof, because individuals in such images can still be identifiable. In my case, I was identified from that very same footage, on-site, within minutes, outside of any formal PDPA process. PDPC then used that same footage to conclude it was not personal data at all.

Since it is the World Cup now, I will borrow an analogy: this is PDPC's Hand of God moment. I asked the referee, PDPC, to review the play. It denied every request to look at the evidence, and let the goal stand, denying me the outcome the rules required. Today's game has VAR, cameras, replay, more evidence than Maradona's referee ever had, and still the referee looked away. Worse, in real football, that referee can be reviewed and held to account by someone else. Here, the enforcer sits in judgment of itself. Nothing happens.

Would you consider submitting a follow-up written question asking how many Access Obligation complaints have been filed with PDPC since 2012, and why zero breach findings have been recorded for that obligation across the entire published enforcement record?

Yours sincerely,
Lim Zirui (Ray Lim)
SMU MBA (Mar 2024, 4.0 GPA); Commendation Medal, Ministry of Health, Covid period.
